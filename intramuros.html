Intramuros

Place: Manila, Philippines

Establishment Date: 1820

What Makes It Unique: The only remaining walled city in the country built by the Spanish in the 16th century and served as the capital of the Philippines for over 300 years. Intramuros is home to a number of historical landmarks, including the Manila Cathedral, the San Agustin Church, and the Fort Santiago.

Overview

Intramuros, meaning "within the walls" in Spanish, is a historic district located in Manila, the capital city of the Philippines. Enclosed by massive stone walls, Intramuros stands as a tangible testament to the country's colonial past and serves as a living heritage site that captures the essence of Spanish influence in the Philippines.

The origins of Intramuros can be traced back to the late 16th century when the Spanish conquistador Miguel López de Legazpi founded the city of Manila. Intramuros was constructed as a fortified city to protect the Spanish settlers from external threats and invasions. The walls, which were built over several decades, enclosed an area of about 64 hectares (158 acres) and served as a symbol of Spanish power and authority.

Throughout its history, Intramuros witnessed significant events that shaped the destiny of the Philippines. The district was the center of political, religious, and economic activities during the Spanish colonial period. The Governor-General's Palace, located within Intramuros, served as the seat of Spanish colonial administration. The area was also home to several churches, monasteries, schools, and institutions that played crucial roles in spreading Christianity and Spanish culture.

Over the centuries, Intramuros faced destruction and reconstruction due to natural disasters and wars. The district suffered heavy damage during the Battle of Manila in 1945, towards the end of World War II, when it became a battleground between Japanese and American forces. The subsequent rebuilding efforts aimed to restore Intramuros to its former glory, preserving its historical and architectural heritage.

Today, Intramuros is a captivating destination that invites visitors to step back in time and immerse themselves in the rich history and cultural legacy of the Philippines. The district boasts a remarkable collection of Spanish colonial architecture, with influences ranging from Moorish to Baroque and Neoclassical styles.

Fort Santiago, a citadel within Intramuros, is one of the district's most prominent landmarks. It served as a military stronghold, a prison, and the site of the imprisonment and execution of national hero Dr. José Rizal. Today, Fort Santiago houses a museum that showcases artifacts and memorabilia related to Rizal's life and the country's struggle for independence.

Exploring Intramuros offers a journey through its charming streets, with horse-drawn carriages known as kalesas adding to the nostalgic ambiance. Visitors can stroll along the cobblestone streets, admire the colonial-era houses, and visit Plaza Roma, a central square adorned with fountains and gardens.

Intramuros hosts various cultural events, festivals, and reenactments, bringing its history to life. The district also offers a vibrant culinary scene, with restaurants and cafes serving both traditional Filipino cuisine and international flavors, allowing visitors to indulge in a culinary exploration.

Intramuros stands as a living testament to the resilience of the Filipino people and their ability to preserve their heritage amidst the challenges of time. It is a captivating destination that invites visitors to delve into the stories, architecture, and traditions that shaped the Philippines during its colonial era. A visit to Intramuros is an opportunity to experience the rich tapestry of history and culture that continues to resonate in the heart of Manila.

Location

Intramuros is easily accessible by public transportation. You can take the LRT or MRT to the Central Terminal Station, and then walk to Intramuros. You can also take a jeepney or bus to Intramuros.


  • From EDSA: Take the MRT to the Central Terminal Station. From there, walk to Intramuros.
  • From Taft Avenue: Take the LRT to the Central Terminal Station. From there, walk to Intramuros.
  • From Quiapo: Take a jeepney or bus to Intramuros.
  • From Makati: Take a jeepney or bus to Intramuros.
