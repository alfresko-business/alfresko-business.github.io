Chocolate Hills

Place: Towns of Carmen, Batuan and Sagbayan, Bohol province

Discovery Date: Unknown

What Makes It Unique: The group of more than 1,200 symmetrical, cone-shaped hills that look like giant chocolate mounds, especially during the dry season when the grass turns brown

History

The Chocolate Hills are a unique geological formation found on the island of Bohol in the Philippines. The hills consist of around 1,776 individual mounds that are evenly distributed across an area of approximately 50 square kilometers. The mounds are conical in shape, and their height ranges from 30 to 50 meters.

The exact formation process of the Chocolate Hills is still a subject of scientific debate, but the most widely accepted theory is that they are the result of the weathering of marine limestone deposits that occurred over millions of years. This theory suggests that the hills were formed during the Pleistocene era, which lasted from about 2.6 million to 11,700 years ago.

According to local folklore, the Chocolate Hills were formed by a giant named Arogo, who fell in love with a mortal woman named Aloya. When Aloya died, Arogo wept for days, and his tears eventually turned into the hills. Another legend suggests that the hills were formed by two feuding giants who threw mud, rocks, and sand at each other until they grew tired and left, leaving behind the landscape.

The Chocolate Hills were first recorded by the Spanish conquistadors who arrived in the Philippines in the 16th century. However, it was not until the early 20th century that the hills gained wider recognition, thanks in part to the efforts of the American governor of Bohol, who included them in the island's tourism promotion campaigns.

Today, the Chocolate Hills are a popular tourist destination and have been declared as a National Geological Monument by the Philippine government. The hills are also a protected area under the National Integrated Protected Areas System, which aims to conserve and protect the natural resources of the country.

In recent years, the Chocolate Hills have faced a number of challenges, including deforestation, mining, and illegal quarrying, which have threatened their beauty and stability. However, efforts are underway to protect the hills and ensure their preservation for future generations to enjoy.

Location

Chocolate Hills can be seen from three different towns of Bohol. For the best viewing experience, it is best to go to the Chocolate Hills View Point located in Carmen, Bohol. From this vantage point, visitors can see the vast expanse of the iconic limestone hills and appreciate their unique geological formation. The deck also offers panoramic views of the surrounding countryside, making it a must-visit destination in the Philippines.
