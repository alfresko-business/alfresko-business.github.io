Hundred Islands National Park

Place: Alaminos, Pangasinan

Establishment Date: January 18, 1940

What Makes It Unique: It is made up of 124 islands, totaling 123 at high tide and 124 at low tide. These islands possess unique shapes and formations, and are believed to have been formed through the erosion of coral reefs over millions of years.

History

Hundred Islands National Park, located in Alaminos City, Pangasinan, Philippines, has a rich and fascinating history. The park's origins can be traced back to the early 1900s when it was initially recognized as a potential tourist destination due to its scenic beauty and natural attractions.

In 1940, then-President Manuel L. Quezon declared Hundred Islands as a national park, making it the first national park in the Philippines. The park was established to protect the unique and diverse ecosystems found within the cluster of islands. It covers an area of approximately 18.44 square kilometers (4,557 acres), encompassing the islands and their surrounding waters.

Over the years, the park has undergone various developments and improvements to enhance its accessibility and tourism potential. Facilities, such as cottages, picnic areas, and viewing decks, were constructed to accommodate visitors and provide amenities for their enjoyment.

To ensure the preservation and sustainable management of the park's natural resources, the local government and environmental agencies have implemented conservation measures. These include strict regulations on fishing, waste management initiatives, and ongoing environmental monitoring and research.

There are only three developed islands for tourists: The Governor Island has an area of 74,408 sq m, here you can find a wide view of the Park from its view deck. There is also a fully furnished 2 bedroom guesthouse with complete amenities which is ideal for family use; Quezon Island has an area of 13,667 sq m servers as a picnic ground and camp site; with an area of 24,053 sq m, Children’s Island offers a panoramic view of the park. There is also a bahay kubos (wooden cottages) for budget travelers who wish to have an overnight stay in the island.

There are caves abound the park, about ten of these caves are open for public exploration. Each cave offers interesting limestone formations such as stalagmites, stalactites, columns and draperies. The caves serve as a dwelling place for bats. The deep sea serves as home to wide varieties of coral reefs which is a home for many species of fish. This also serves as a great site for kayaking, swimming and snorkeling. Visiting the park is one way of just being with nature and admiring the wonders of it.

Today, Hundred Islands National Park remains a popular tourist destination, attracting both local and international visitors. Island hopping tours, snorkeling, diving, and camping are among the activities offered, allowing visitors to explore the diverse landscapes, pristine waters, and vibrant marine life that the park has to offer.

Location

To reach Hundred Islands National Park. From Cubao, take a bus bound for Alaminos, Pangasinan. Fare is about ₱393 ($8) for airconditioned bus and roughly ₱350 ($7) for ordinary bus. Travel time is approximately 5 hours.


Upon arrival in Alaminos, take a tricycle that will bring you to Lucap Wharf in Brgy. Lucap. Tricycle fare is around 80-100 pesos per ride that can fit around 4 persons per tricycle.


At Lucap Wharf, go to the tourism office to rent a boat for your island hopping adventure in Hundred Islands National Park. Boat rental (check rates below) depends on the size of the boat and whether your tour is day tour or overnight.
